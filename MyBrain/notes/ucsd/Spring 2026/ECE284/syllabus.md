Course Summary

In this class you will be introduced to the broad research field of digital health technologies through discussions of leading research and a self selected hands-on project. The field of digital health technologies (DHT) spans many disciplines, and rather than any specific technical solution space, it is more of an application domain. In this class, we’ll largely focus on health monitoring in non-clinical spaces through the use of mobile and wearable technologies. But at times, we will also discuss topics around digital technologies meant for the delivery of healthcare. This course assumes a basic understanding of electronics, signal processing, and data processing techniques, but prerequisite courses are not required. Individuals missing background information may require additional study during the course to understand the material and are encouraged to reach out to the teaching team for additional resources.

This course is organized as a set of required readings and in-class discussions. To promote lively and interesting discussion, the discussions will be student led, with participation from others in the class. Readings focus on a variety of topics including health specific topics (i.e. sleep, women’s health, public health) to technology specific topics (i.e. LLMs, Non-contact Sensing, Large scale wearable data). The goal of this class is to provide you with a breadth of exposure to the open world that is Digital Health Technologies.

This course will also involve a quarter long self-guided individual project. Each student will have to propose a project based on their own interest/skillset. Students are encouraged to bring existing research projects and apply the course to it if applicable. It may be hardware or software, big data or small data, a research project that is on-going or a project you’re just wanting to try out. If something is really interesting and you need help getting access to resources to complete it, talk to the professor and TA, we may be able to help you secure it.

# Primary Discussion Leading

Sign up: Each paper discussion will be led by 2-3 students. Sign up is first come first serve in the reading list. You are only allowed to sign up for 1 paper in the first half (weeks 2-6) and one paper in the second half (weeks 6-10). Do not sign up for multiple papers on week 6. You may only be allowed to sign up as a 3rd student on a discussion lead once all papers have at least 2 primary leads. Otherwise, we’ll move you to a paper at random.

Discussion Leading: Each paper will be allotted 20 minutes for presentation and discussion. The primary discussion leads are to put together a 7-10 minute presentation about the key points of the paper then facilitate the discussion. As the discussion lead, students are expected to read the entire paper. Just skimming and listening to AI summaries will be insufficient. If there are multiple discussion leaders, each person is expected to lead the discussion evenly. Note: Grades for discussion leading will be individual.

Presentation Content: Your slides should cover the following, roughly in order:

- Problem & Motivation — Why does this problem matter? Who is affected? What is the clinical or societal gap?
- Technical Approach — What did the authors actually build or do? Go beyond restating the abstract. Explain the key insight or design choice that makes their approach different.
- Key Results — Identify the 2-3 most important figures or tables. Explain what they show and why they matter. Do not just screenshot figures from the paper; annotate or redraw them to highlight your interpretation.
- Limitations & Open Questions — What did the authors miss, understate, or leave unresolved?

Discussion Questions: Lead discussion around 4 questions in each of the following categories (label them accordingly):

1. Clinical/Real-world Relevance — Would this work outside the lab? Who benefits, who is excluded, what breaks in deployment? (Example: "The study only recruited healthy 20-30 year olds. How would aging or darker skin tones affect signal quality?")
2. Methodological Critique — Is the evaluation convincing? What is the weakest assumption, dataset limitation, or statistical gap? (Example: "They report mean absolute error but not per-subject variance. Could a few outliers be driving the result?")
3. Technical Extension — How could you build on this work? What is the next experiment or design iteration? (Example: "They used a CNN. Would a foundation model pretrained on wearable time-series data improve generalization?")
4. Broader Implications — Ethics, equity, privacy, regulation, policy, or connection to another paper in this course. (Example: "If this ships as a consumer app without FDA clearance, who is liable for a missed diagnosis?")

Questions that are generic (e.g., "What did you think of the paper?") or surface-level (e.g., "Was the sample size big enough?") without specific grounding in the paper will not receive full credit. The questions should be back up questions for if the course discussion is dying off.

During Discussion: You are evaluated on your ability to facilitate discussion, not just read questions aloud. This means calling on classmates, following up on their responses, pushing back constructively, and briefly synthesizing across responses before moving to the next question. If there are multiple discussion leaders, each person is expected to lead discussion evenly. The recommended strategy for leading the discussion evenly is to split up leads for discussion questions. Grades are individual.

Meaningful participation will be difficult without knowing the papers. At minimum, students are expected to read the abstracts of ALL papers. Students will additionally read/skim 2 papers a week for the biweekly oral assessments. ## Oral Assessments

This course uses oral assessments conducted by a conversation agent built by the instructional team. The purpose of oral assessments is to evaluate your understanding of the material, not your ability to produce polished written or recorded deliverables. You will have a conversation with the agent, which will ask you questions and follow up based on your responses.

There are three types of oral assessments:

Primary Discussion Paper Assessment (15-20 minutes): For each paper you lead as a primary discussant, you will complete an oral assessment. This is a comprehensive conversation that will probe your understanding of the paper's motivation, methodology, results, and limitations. Expect follow-up questions that go beyond what was covered in your presentation. You should be able to explain the paper's technical details, justify the authors' design choices, and articulate what you would do differently. The first primary assessment needs to be completed by the end of Week 6 and the second primary assessment needs to be completed by the end of Week 10. 

Biweekly Paper Reading (Secondary) Assessment (5-10 minutes): For 2 papers per week, you will complete a shorter oral assessment. This replaces the course’s previous written reflection. The goal is for students to become proficient at understanding the key concepts of papers in an efficient manner. Skimming the paper and utilizing AI tools is encouraged. The conversational agent will ask you to demonstrate that you read the paper and engaged with it critically. This needs to be completed BEFORE the class discussions for credit. If the readings of the week are not discussed the week they are assigned (like week 2), the readings will be due midnight on Friday of that week with a 2 day grace period for late work. These assessments are meant to prepare you for the week’s discussions. As such these assignments are due at the beginning of the week (Week 2 oral assessments are due midnight Monday of week 2, week 3 oral assessments are due Monday of week 3, etc.).

Final Project Assessment (defense): Instead of a recorded presentation, your final project will be assessed through an oral defense conducted by the conversation agent. The agent will ask you to explain your project's motivation, methodology, results, and evaluation. You should be prepared to justify your design decisions, discuss limitations, and respond to critical questions about your work. This is the primary way we assess your final project, so come prepared to speak in depth about every aspect of it. Oral assessment for the final project will be available starting Monday of finals week. This is to ensure enough time to prepare the oral assessments after final reports have been turned in (Friday of Week 10).

# Final Project

As a core part of this class, you are to develop a final project on the topic of digital health technologies. The sky's the limit and you are not bound to working on any specific subset of digital health technology topics. You may draw inspiration from the papers we discuss, from your own research, or perhaps articles you’ve come across. Because this is not a technical skill driven class, it also means that you are expected to draw from your own background, may it be hardware or software (or both). Although a large part of the class revolves around monitoring health, a number of papers also focus on behavioral interventions or even the use and design of technologies.

Project Proposal

Part of the learning process is to build up the ability to seek out interesting ideas to work on, but it may not come naturally. Feel free to reach out to the professor, brainstorm with others in the class, look on the internet. If you are having trouble coming up with an idea, a good project could be to find a paper with an open dataset and replicate the results. The project does not have to be novel, what is important is that you can elaborate clearly the motivation/background behind the work, the methods you’ve used, how it compares with other existing works, and interpret your results.

You are to give a 3-5 minute oral presentation with 1-3 slides describing the problem you want to address and a basic idea around how you plan to approach it. Be sure that your presentation clearly describes the motivation (specifically the health topic), what existing approaches exists, what you want to accomplish in your project (i.e. build something new, replicate a promising study), what will you need to accomplish it (i.e. dataset, hardware).

Project Report

The project report will be a formalized report of the updated project proposal. In the week following the presentation you should talk to at least 3 classmates about your project and include feedback summaries in a supplemental page to your report. The project report should be 2 pages (with an additional supplemental page on the feedback summaries). The project report should cover the motivation of the project, the proposed goal, and a detailed plan including a timeline.

Note that if you want to propose the use of resources you don’t have access to, be sure to check with the instructional team to see if it is something that may be possible to obtain. If not, you may have to try something else. Do this BEFORE the project report.

Project Update

You are to submit a written 2 page update on the project. This report should provide a brief summary of the problem you are approaching, but focus mostly on the progress you have made so far. Grading will be based on a clear demonstration of progress around the planned method, obtaining the necessary resources to conduct the project, early prototype visuals, etc. Just describing a plan for the rest of the quarter is not enough.

You should have at least 1-2 figures (i.e. software architecture, system diagram, sketch/mock up, plots of initial result) that help to visualize your progress. DO NOT show a screenshot of your code/terminal output as an update.  

Final Report

The final report will be a 7-10 page report (excluding references) that follows the [ACM Large 2 column](https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.overleaf.com/latex/templates/association-for-computing-machinery-acm-large-2-column-format-template/qwcgpbmkkvpq&sa=D&source=editors&ust=1776649993202950&usg=AOvVaw3YP6FxpYy1CA3NabcQDecd) format provided by the ACM. The report should have clearly marked sections of Motivation, Related Work, Project Aim, Methodology, Results, Discussion, and Conclusion. The conclusion should be a clear summary of what you did and the main take away.

You will be graded based on clarity of information presentation, judicious use of visuals, argument based on logic and data supporting the claims.

Some projects will be ambitious. We encourage you to do them. It will be likely that you won’t be able to make the “perfect” version by the end of the quarter. That is OK. However, you should still be able to provide all the details to show how you’re approaching the problem. You can also show interim results. If your system is to produce some kind of vital sign, even if it doesn’t work perfectly, you should come up with some way to show what you’ve been able to achieve. If you’ve built a mobile app to perform an intervention, ideally you should try deploying it on a few people. But if you couldn’t, then you should have very good descriptions on how the system would have worked. It is YOUR job to show us how the system works and what it can do.

Practice good documentation and version control with your submissions. Include a Github repository of your work with a README describing the codebase. This should be relatively easy with LLM support.Note that if your documentation is very poorly formatted, for example having massive white spaces, such as placing a single image on a page, unnecessarily large images, etc, you will be marked down for formatting.

# Policy on AI Use

Advances in available AI tools are quickly improving and learning to use these tools effectively is important. We encourage utilizing novel technologies to facilitate learning in this course. Using AI tools to summarize research papers, complete project tasks, or generate presentation slides is allowed. Students must be honest about the use of AI. The project report, update, and final report should clearly state to what extent AI tools were used to complete various tasks. Please consider that AI generated content can be incorrect or off topic so it is important to check AI generated work. This will be particularly true for presentation slides. It is the student’s responsibility to understand the limitations of the tools. AI tools or any external resources are NOT allowed for any oral assessments.
